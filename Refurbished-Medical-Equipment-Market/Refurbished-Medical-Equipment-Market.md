<p>One of the big challenges the market for refurbished medical equipment confronts is complying with the highly regulated environment. Health authorities, such as FDA in the U.S., CE in Europe, and other such regulators worldwide, have stringent standards for medical devices. For safety to the patients and compliance with regulations, reconditioned devices must be equal in quality, performance, and safety requirements as the device is while new.</p>
<p>It can be costly and time-consuming to renovate medical devices to these stringent requirements. It tends to take special equipment and staff and entails serious inspection, testing, and certification. Further, individuals who sell refurbished equipment must keep up with changing regulatory regulations so that their devices remain up to date under current rules.<br />On other fronts, there are also limits on what types of equipment may be refurbished and resold, and certain equipment can be prohibited or limited based on age, state of repair, or technology. To doctors, the inconvenience of going through all these regulatory challenges may deter them from buying used equipment, but it is highly cost-saving. While reconditioned medical equipment is an important factor in expanding access to health care, especially in poor areas, compliance is the problem. Care providers should take special precautions to ensure that their refurbishment practices meet all regulations to reduce risk to patient safety, legal liability, and potential fines. But this issue also presents an opportunity for companies to differentiate themselves by providing certified, high-quality reconditioned equipment that meets all applicable legal requirements.</p>
